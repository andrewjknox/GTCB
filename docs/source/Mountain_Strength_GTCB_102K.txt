How to use this book
A finish-focused mountain ultra is won by legs that still brake on the descents at hour 18 and a body that
stays upright and loaded for 24 hours. This strength work serves that — not aesthetics, not max numbers.
READ THIS FIRST — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE
Alex Effer's prescribed corrective work is the backbone of your lower-leg and left-chain training. This
book is a general strength supplement that sits around his rehab, never over the top of it. Where
exercises here touch the calf or the left kinetic chain (foot → tibia → hip → knee → calf), his guidance
wins every time. Run any new calf loading past him before adding volume — your working picture is
plantarflexor hyperexcitability / EAMC, not a tissue you simply load harder. 
The principles
One quality session per week, ~45–55 min including warm-up, on your erg/rowing day. Rowing is
general aerobic and posterior-biased, so strength rounds out what the erg misses: single-leg control,
eccentric braking, lateral chain, trunk endurance.
Rotate the emphasis week to week (Templates 1 → 2 → 3 → repeat). Because it's only once weekly,
each session is full-body with a different lean, not a body-part split.
Eccentric / descent-protective work is the priority bucket. If a session gets cut short, keep
those movements.
Quality over load. Leave 2–3 reps in reserve (RIR). You're building durable tissue and control, not
chasing a 1RM. Sore legs must not compromise the Friday Peak day.
Periodise down into the race. Heaviest in base (Jun–Aug), trimmed through build, maintenance-
only from ~mid-Oct so it never blunts your peak mountain weeks.
Never stack heavy lower-body the day before a long Peak day. Keep strength early-week.

Exercise library
Reference for every movement used in the templates. Tags show the primary purpose; doses are starting
points for the base phase — progress per the rules at the back.
A · Descent-protective & eccentric priority bucket
Eccentric split squatA1
PRIORITYSINGLE-LEG
The single best gym mimic of descending: loaded knee flexion under a slow, controlled lower. Builds
quads' braking capacity so they survive 6,280m of downhill.
Setup: Split stance, rear foot on the floor (or lightly on a low bench). Torso tall, weight through the front
mid-foot.
Execution: Lower slowly and under control, front knee tracking over toes, until the front thigh is near
parallel. Drive up at normal speed. The lowering is the whole point.
TEMPO
4-1-1-0
DOSE
3 × 6–8 / leg
PROGRESS
Hold dumbbells → slower eccentric
Watch: Keep the knee stable, no inward collapse — that's your left-chain pattern showing up. Stop the
set if control goes.
Step-down (forward & lateral)A2
PRIORITYSINGLE-LEG
Directly trains the controlled, single-leg lowering of a downhill foot-plant, plus the hip/knee control that
stops the left knee hyperextending and the foot over-pronating.
Setup: Stand on a step/box (start ~15 cm, build height). One leg working, other leg hanging off the
edge.
Execution: Slowly lower the free heel toward the floor by bending the standing leg — tap lightly, don't
dump weight onto it — then control back up. Lateral version: step off to the side to load the hip
abductors / lateral chain.
TEMPO
3-1-1-0
DOSE
3 × 8 / leg
PROGRESS
Higher box → light DB
Watch: Hips level (no drop on the free side), knee over mid-foot. This is a control drill before it's a
strength drill.

Spanish squat (isometric)A3
PRIORITY
Knee-friendly quad loading with low joint stress — builds tendon-tolerant quad strength without the
impact, useful when legs are already loaded from hill days.
Setup: Loop a stout band around a fixed post and behind both knees. Lean back into the band, feet
planted.
Execution: Sit back into a squat (~60–90° knee bend) with shins vertical and hold. Quads do the work;
the band takes the shear.
HOLD
Iso :30–:45
DOSE
4–5 holds
PROGRESS
Add held weight / longer hold
Reverse NordicA4
PRIORITY
Loaded eccentric lengthening of the quads through a big range — potent protection against late-race
downhill quad blow-up. Advanced; ease in.
Setup: Kneel tall on a pad, knees hip-width, torso and hips in one line.
Execution: Keeping a straight line from knee to head, lean back slowly under control as far as you can
hold, then return. Small range first; build over weeks.
TEMPO
4-0-2-0
DOSE
2–3 × 5–8
PROGRESS
Greater lean angle
Watch: No hinging at the hip (don't fold) and no lower-back arch. Quads only. Start tiny — this gets sore.
Eccentric heel dropA5
CALF · CHECK EFFER
Classic calf/Achilles eccentric loading. Relevant to descending and push-off — but this is exactly the tissue
under your working diagnosis.
EFFER GOVERNS THIS ONE
Do not add eccentric calf loading on your own initiative. Use only the heel-drop volume, range
and frequency Effer has set, and the cues he's given (heel elevation, big-toe wedge, breathing).
The notes below are descriptive, not a prescription. 
Execution (if cleared): Rise on both feet, shift to the working leg, lower the heel slowly below a step.
Soleus bias: same with the knee bent.

B · Single-leg strength & stability
Bulgarian split squatB1
SINGLE-LEG
The workhorse single-leg strength builder. Mountain running is one leg at a time on uneven ground — this
builds the unilateral strength and balance bilateral squats miss.
Setup: Rear foot elevated on a bench, front foot far enough forward that the knee stays over mid-foot at
the bottom.
Execution: Lower under control until the front thigh is ~parallel, drive through the front heel/mid-foot to
stand. Keep the torso tall-ish.
TEMPO
3-0-1-0
DOSE
3 × 8–10 / leg
PROGRESS
DBs → goblet → slow tempo
Watch left side: hip stays level, knee doesn't dive in, no rotation. Quality over load on the weaker
chain.
High step-upB2
SINGLE-LEG
The closest gym mirror of power-hiking a steep climb — the GTCB's bread and butter. Trains hip/knee
extension from a deep, loaded position.
Setup: Box at mid-thigh height (knee bent ~90° when the foot's on it).
Execution: Drive up through the top leg with minimal push-off from the floor leg — make the working
leg do it. Control the lower back down (don't just drop).
TEMPO
2-0-2-0
DOSE
3 × 8 / leg
PROGRESS
Hold DBs / loaded pack
Loading with your race pack ties it straight to power-hiking specificity.
Single-leg Romanian deadliftB3
SINGLE-LEGPOSTERIOR
Builds the glute/hamstring chain unilaterally and trains balance + hip control on one leg — exactly the
stability you need on technical, fatigued descents.
Setup: Stand on one leg, soft knee, weight in the opposite hand (or both).
Execution: Hinge at the hip, back flat, free leg extending behind as a counterweight; lower until you feel
the hamstring load, return by squeezing the glute.
TEMPO
3-0-1-0
DOSE
3 × 8 / leg
PROGRESS
Heavier DB / KB
Watch: Hips square to the floor (resist the open-up rotation) — this directly trains against your left-chain
pattern.

C · Posterior chain & glutes
Romanian deadlift (bilateral)C1
POSTERIOR
Strong hamstrings and glutes power the climbs and protect the knees on descents. The hip hinge also
reinforces the posture you'll hold for 24 hours.
Execution: Barbell or DBs; hinge at the hips with a flat back, bar close to the legs, lower to mid-shin or
where the back stays neutral, drive hips forward to stand.
TEMPO
3-0-1-0
DOSE
3 × 8–10
PROGRESS
Add load, keep form
Hip thrust / glute bridgeC2
POSTERIOR
Direct glute strength — the prime mover for climbing and the muscle that should fire first in your chain.
Strong glutes take load off the over-eager calf.
Execution: Upper back on a bench (thrust) or floor (bridge), barbell/DB across the hips, drive through
heels to full hip extension, squeeze glutes hard at the top, lower with control.
TEMPO
2-1-1-0
DOSE
3 × 10–12
PROGRESS
Load → single-leg
Banded lateral walk / clamshellC3
POSTERIORLATERAL CHAIN
Targets glute medius — the hip stabiliser that keeps your pelvis level and stops the left-side collapse
pattern on every single foot-plant over 102km.
Execution: Band above knees or ankles. Lateral walk: half-squat, step sideways keeping tension, hips
level. Clamshell: side-lying, knees bent, open the top knee without rolling the pelvis back.
DOSE (WALK)
3 × 10 steps / way
DOSE (CLAM)
3 × 12–15 / side
PROGRESS
Stronger band
Doubles as warm-up activation. Useful most sessions.

D · Trunk & postural endurance
A 24-hour race is a posture-endurance event. These build a trunk that holds you upright and stable when everything's
tired — anti-movement > crunches.
Suitcase carry (loaded carry)D1
TRUNK
Anti-lateral-flexion under load while walking — almost perfect transfer to staying tall and stable under a
pack for hours. Underrated for ultras.
Execution: Heavy DB/KB in one hand, walk tall and level, resisting the lean toward the weight. Switch
sides.
DOSE
3 × ~30m / side
CUE
Ribs down, hips level
PROGRESS
Heavier / longer
Pallof pressD2
TRUNK
Anti-rotation — trains the trunk to resist the twist that creeps in with fatigue and feeds compensatory
chains. Good carryover to your left-side rotation tendency.
Execution: Band/cable at chest height to the side. Press the handle straight out and resist being
rotated, hold, return. Stay square.
DOSE
3 × 10 / side
CUE
Slow, no twist
PROGRESS
Stronger band / longer hold
Side plank (+ variations)D3
TRUNKLATERAL CHAIN
Lateral trunk + glute medius endurance in one. Holds the pelvis stable late in the race when the left side
wants to give.
Execution: Forearm under shoulder, body in a line, hips up. Build from knees → full → top-leg raises.
Hold steady, breathe.
DOSE
3 × :30–:45 / side
PROGRESS
Longer hold / leg raise

Dead bug & bird dogD4
TRUNK
Trunk control with limb movement and breathing — fits naturally with Effer's breathing-led approach.
Teaches a stable core while arms and legs work, which is what running actually is.
Dead bug: on your back, low back gently pressed down, slowly lower opposite arm + leg, exhale, return.
Bird dog: on hands and knees, extend opposite arm + leg without tipping, hold, switch.
DOSE
2–3 × 8 / side
CUE
Slow + exhale on reach
PROGRESS
Slower / longer levers
E · Calf & lower leg Effer-led zone
WHOLE SECTION GOVERNED BY EFFER
Your calf is the limiter and the working diagnosis (plantarflexor hyperexcitability / EAMC on the
altered-neuromuscular-control model) means more calf load is not automatically better. Treat
this section as a checklist of what calf work exists, not licence to program it yourself. Volume,
progression and whether to load at all are Effer's calls. Build strength without tipping into the over-
firing pattern — and respect your own flagged tendency to over-test. 
Seated calf raise (soleus)E1
CALF · CHECK EFFER
Soleus is the endurance calf muscle and a big player in distance running and descending control. Bent-
knee position biases it.
Execution: Seated, knees bent ~90°, weight on thighs, raise through the balls of the feet, full controlled
lower. Heel-elevation and big-toe cues per Effer.
Only at the dose Effer sets.
Standing calf raise (gastrocnemius)E2
CALF · CHECK EFFER
Straight-knee calf raise biases the gastroc — the power calf for push-off. Single-leg builds the side-to-side
balance running demands.
Execution: Standing, knees straight, rise high, lower slowly with control. Progress bilateral → single-leg
only when cleared.
Only at the dose Effer sets. Stop at any sign of the cramp/over-firing pattern.

Progression rules (base & build)
Add reps before load. Hit the top of the rep range with 2–3 RIR for two sessions, then nudge
weight up and drop back to the bottom of the range.
Slow the tempo as a progression in its own right — a 5-second eccentric is harder and more
descent-specific than just more weight.
Left side leads the dose. On single-leg work, the weaker/affected side sets the reps and load; the
strong side matches, not exceeds.
One variable at a time. Don't add load, reps and range in the same week.
When to back off (no heroics)
Any hint of the calf cramp / over-firing pattern → stop calf work, tell Effer.
Strength soreness bleeding into the Friday Peak day → cut volume or move the session earlier in the
week.
Left knee/hip control degrading mid-set → end the set; it's a quality drill, not an ego one.
Big mountain B2B coming → downgrade to Template 4/5 that week. The race is built on the hills, not
the gym.
THE ONE-LINE SUMMARY
Strength here is insurance for the descents and the left chain, and an engine for the climbs — a
supporting act to your hills, bike, row and Effer's rehab. Keep it consistent, keep it light enough to
never cost you a Peak day, and taper it out so you arrive in Finestrat fresh.
